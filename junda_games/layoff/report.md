### David Begnaud Pioneers Hybrid Journalism Model with New Media Company and CBS Role
- **Source:** The Guardian
- **Published:** 2026-02-24T08:13:17+00:00
- **Summary:** CBS News personality David Begnaud is embracing a pioneering hybrid model, launching his independent media company, Do Good Crew, while notably retaining his day job as a CBS contributor. This move diverges from a trend where many journalists leave stable roles for independent ventures like Substack or beehiiv, as the industry shifts towards individual personalities.

Begnaud’s Do Good Crew will offer a weekly newsletter powered by beehiiv, a podcast titled "The Person Who Believed in Me With David Begnaud" — launching with Oprah Winfrey — and a live events series. This initiative stems from his passion for positive storytelling, inspired by his CBS segment. He prioritized maintaining his relationship with CBS News, valuing the community he built, and CBS leadership, including President Tom Cibrowski, has reportedly supported his vision as a partner.

This dual-role approach is championed by Wendy McMahon, former CBS News president and current senior adviser to beehiiv. McMahon advocates for the future of journalism to involve institutions and independent creators collaborating. She argues legacy newsrooms offer essential scale, news-gathering resources, and standards, while the independent sphere provides innovation, intimacy, and a redefined sense of community.

Beehiiv, the platform underpinning Begnaud’s newsletter, facilitates this model. It charges a user fee instead of taking a cut of subscription revenue and provides journalists in its media collective with crucial benefits like health insurance, wire images, and legal services—perks typically associated with traditional employers. Tyler Denk, beehiiv's co-founder, highlights the platform’s flexible design for creator growth and monetization. Begnaud views this changing landscape as an "immense, incredible opportunity," enabling him to tell impactful stories and collaborate with like-minded brands.

### Block Slashes 4,000 Jobs, CEO Jack Dorsey Cites AI Efficiency Gains
- **Source:** The Guardian
- **Published:** 2026-02-28T08:13:17+00:00
- **Summary:** Fintech giant Block, the parent company of Square and Cash App, announced a substantial workforce reduction, laying off 4,000 of its 10,000 employees. CEO Jack Dorsey explicitly attributed these drastic cuts to advancements in artificial intelligence productivity, asserting that "intelligence tools have changed what it means to build and run a company." He explained that a significantly smaller team, utilizing AI, can achieve more and perform better, with AI capabilities rapidly compounding. Dorsey framed this decisive action as a proactive measure to prevent prolonged, morale-damaging incremental layoffs, despite the company's strong economic performance, having surpassed Wall Street's fourth-quarter expectations. Investors reacted positively, with Block's shares surging over 20% in pre-market trading.

Block's move serves as a prominent real-world example amid broader societal concerns about AI's impact on employment. Experts like Goldman Sachs have warned of AI's potential to increase unemployment, estimating 5,000 to 10,000 monthly net job losses last year, while an MIT study suggested AI could displace nearly 12% of the US workforce. The tech sector, in particular, is experiencing similar trends, with companies such as Salesforce also reducing staff due to AI efficiencies.

While Block has integrated AI for years, the significant layoffs follow earlier cuts and coincide with reports of rapidly deteriorating employee morale and a "crumbling" company culture, partly due to mandates for generative AI. Dorsey acknowledged the inherent risks of this AI-driven strategy; Block's 10-K filing cautions that the effectiveness and reliability of these tools are crucial for the reduced workforce model and could introduce operational or cybersecurity challenges. Analysts highlight Block's direct and public attribution of job cuts to AI, a stance differentiating it from other companies that have been less explicit about technology's role in workforce reductions.
- [Link to Article](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/feb/27/block-ai-layoffs-jack-dorsey)

However, Melissa Otto of S&P Global emphasizes that growing AI capital expenditure and rapid advances threaten existing business models, questioning how software companies will compete. She suggests firms with complex workflows and proprietary, predictive data will hold a competitive edge. Consequently, investors are now flocking to "safe haven" consumer staples. While some analysts, like RBC Capital Markets' Lori Calvasina, believe certain "existential concerns" for non-software industries might be "overblown," the current market climate reflects a profound re-evaluation of AI's economic implications beyond its initial hype.
